Manifesto

18 principles for technology that serves your mission

Technology is never neutral.

For social purpose organisations, the tools you choose shape who holds power, who can participate, and who gets left vulnerable. Convenience has a cost: in data harvested, in decisions outsourced to distant platforms, in communities made dependent on infrastructure they cannot see or influence.

TechFreedom exists to make those costs visible — and to support organisations that want their technology to match their values. This manifesto sets out what that means in practice.

The Six Themes

01
01 — Jurisdiction

Where power sits

  • Data should be subject to laws that respect the rights and safety of the people it concerns.
  • Organisations should know where their data actually lives and under whose authority it can be accessed.
  • Critical data should not be governed solely by jurisdictions whose interests conflict with the communities served.
02
02 — Business Continuity

Who can switch off your work

  • No single company should have the power to stop an organisation working overnight.
  • Essential services should have clear exit paths, backups, and alternatives.
  • Contracts and terms of service should never be the only place where continuity is considered.
03
03 — Surveillance

What the stack knows and extracts

  • People have a right to organise, learn, and receive services without being tracked beyond what is necessary.
  • Analytics should serve the mission of the organisation, not the advertising and data-mining needs of third parties.
  • Tools that treat users as raw material for surveillance capitalism have no place at the heart of social purpose work.
04
04 — Lock-in

How free you are to leave

  • Organisations should be able to leave any platform without threat or punishment.
  • Open standards, data portability, and interoperability are baseline requirements, not optional extras.
  • A tool that is hard to leave is a risk, no matter how polished its interface may be.
05
05 — Cost Exposure

Who carries the financial shock

  • Long-term affordability matters more than introductory discounts and “free” tiers.
  • Budget decisions should be based on realistic scenarios about future pricing and licensing changes.
  • The true cost of a tool includes migration, training, and the energy spent working around its limitations.

Commitment

  • Technology choices are political choices.
  • Every organisation can move, step by step, towards a stack that is more transparent, accountable, and aligned with its purpose.
  • TechFreedom stands with those who want to make that journey — together rather than alone.
